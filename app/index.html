What Is This?

Refugee Community on Slack is a central platform for individuals, groups and organizations to communicate to one another and help solve the refugee crisis.

Who Is This For?

  • Volunteers
  • Refugees
  • Charities
  • Entrepreneurs
  • Government Staff

How Did This Community Begin?

The idea for this community began when a young couple heard about Refugees Welcome, a project in Austria and Germany that pairs refugees with accommodation in shared flats. John and Sophie Ellison had just moved to the UK and wanted to have a refugee live with them in their home.

Contacting Refugees Welcome

When the Ellisons contacted Refugees Welcome, it took weeks to get a response. The organization was overloaded with requests from hundreds of people asking if they could help set up Refugees Welcome in their country.

A Refugees Welcome representative finally got back in touch with The Ellisons and introduced them to a group of individuals in the UK who also wanted to help refugees.

A Scattered Conversation

In the weeks that ensued, thousands of emails were exchanged between caring people from across the UK who wanted to help setup a Refugees Welcome branch in England. As Refugees Welcome gained publicity, the list of participants grew and the conversation scattered across several different platforms: Facebook, Google, and Skype—just to name a few. It was difficult for John and Sophie to keep up with the conversation because they had full-time jobs and there was no structure or order to the conversation.

Inspiration

Inspired by John Willshire's Popular Thing for Broken Thing workshop, John Ellison had an idea to take a popular thing (Slack—a successful messaging app) to help fix a broken thing (the refugee crisis). In pairing two sticky notes together, the concept for Refugee Community on Slack was born. You can read more about the workshop on John's 90 Day blog.

The Refugee Community Concept

The idea was to create a central forum for all of the conversations about the refugee crisis. Whether people wanted to create a Refugee Welcome branch in their own country or just wanted to have a refugee in their home, the Refugee Community on Slack would allow for people to ask questions, exchange ideas and help build a community of people who wanted to make a difference amidst the refugee crisis.

Imitation As A Compliment

John Ellison began using Slack in his work as a UX Designer and it was an amazing communication tool for teams working on complex projects. In working at Clearleft, John was introduced to the UserExperience Design channel on Slack by a co-worker.

The User Experience Design channel on Slack is a great example of caring people from all over the world coming together to help one another. There are over 5,000 designers that make up the community. It is a vibrant forum full of amazing conversations from beginners to experts and everything in between.

Where Are We Now?

The Refugee Community on Slack is in its infancy. John Ellison has created the Slack channel and launched this landing page. He is personally inviting people to the community one-by-one and trying to understand the diverse needs of people from all over the world who want to help with the refugee crisis.

We Need Your Help

There are hundreds of thousands of refugees that need homes.

There are hundreds of thousands of people suffering without access to food and clean water.

There are thousands of people who are risking their lives to migrate to Europe.

There are organizations around the world that desperately need volunteers.

There are millions of citizens who have spare bedrooms that want to have refugees come and live with them.

There are billions of dollars coming in from around the world to help with the refugee crisis.

We need your help.

As with any complex problem, we need a diverse group of passionate people who want to help break this problem into smaller chunks and create innovative solutions.

Whether you're an entrepreneur, a writer, designer, developer, government official, student, or just someone who wants to help—join our community and offer your help to those in need.

If you're interested in helping with build this online community, the best way to start is by joining the slack channel. If you've already submitted a request for an invitation, you can reach out to John Ellison on Twitter: @iamjohnellison.

The Time is Now

This is a unique moment in history. The problems we are facing are not new. We have seen wars, genocide, disease and famine before. We have seen natural disasters, discrimination and murder. We have seen it all.

But now—more than ever before—we have the power to solve problems. The power is at our fingertips. We have access to world's information at the palm of our hands. We can learn how to do anything. We can speak with anyone. We can share what we have learned and people will listen.

The digital era is changing our world. Technology and design together have transformed life as we know it. While many of these innovations have made our lives better and some have made them worse, we are just beginning to see the potential of technology and design to make an impact and help real people with real problems. The question then becomes how?

The answer lies in working together. Together, we are better. We can help the Syrian refugee crisis. Together we can understand the diverse needs of people from all over the world who are suffering and fleeing from persecution. If we work together we can help make a difference.

We cannot back and watch while the rest of the world takes action. We cannot sit back and watch while the trains full of men, women and children roll by the countryside. We cannot sit back and watch the gray clouds of black smoke fill up the silent blue sky.
